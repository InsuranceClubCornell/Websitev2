Career Roadmap

Six Paths into the Industry

Whether you're drawn to pricing risk, managing claims, or placing coverage, there's a career track built for your skills and interests.

Underwriting ↗ Deep Dive

Assess and price risk across P&C, life, health, and specialty lines. Master policy forms, risk selection, and portfolio management.

P&CSpecialtyCyberLife & Health
Actuarial Science ↗ Deep Dive

Use mathematics and statistics to quantify risk and set reserves. The most quantitative path — pursue the FSA or FCAS designation.

FSAFCASModelingReserving
Broking & Advisory ↗ Deep Dive

Represent clients in the insurance marketplace, placing complex risks and structuring bespoke coverage programs at firms like Marsh, Aon, and WTW.

PlacementClient RelationsWholesale
Claims Management ↗ Deep Dive

Investigate and resolve insurance claims fairly and efficiently. A high-judgment role at the intersection of coverage analysis, negotiation, and customer service.

Coverage AnalysisLitigationNegotiation
Investment Management ↗ Deep Dive

Manage the float — the premiums collected before claims are paid. Insurance companies are among the largest institutional investors in the world.

Fixed IncomeALMPortfolio
InsurTech & Analytics ↗ Deep Dive

Harness data science, AI, and technology to transform how risk is modeled, priced, and distributed. The fastest-growing corner of the industry.

Data ScienceAI/MLProduct

Glossary

Know the Language

From loss ratios to cedants — master the terminology that sets professionals apart in any interview room.

Loss Ratio
Underwriting · Core

Incurred losses divided by earned premiums. The primary measure of underwriting profitability — a ratio below 100% means claims cost less than premiums collected.

Combined Ratio
Underwriting · Core

Loss ratio plus expense ratio. Below 100% indicates underwriting profit. Most carriers target a combined ratio between 90–99%.

Cedant
Reinsurance

An insurance company that transfers (cedes) a portion of its risk to a reinsurer. The primary insurer in a reinsurance arrangement.

Treaty Reinsurance
Reinsurance

A standing reinsurance agreement that automatically covers a portfolio of risks, as opposed to facultative reinsurance which covers individual risks.

Adverse Selection
Underwriting

The tendency for higher-risk individuals to seek more insurance coverage. A fundamental challenge in pricing — bad risks gravitate toward cheap coverage.

Moral Hazard
Risk Management

The risk that having insurance reduces the incentive to prevent losses. A key reason for deductibles, co-pays, and coverage sublimits in policy design.

IBNR Reserve
Actuarial

Incurred But Not Reported reserves — funds held for claims that have occurred but haven't yet been filed. A critical actuarial judgment item on the balance sheet.

Catastrophe (Cat) Modeling
Actuarial · Underwriting

Probabilistic modeling of large-scale disaster events (hurricanes, earthquakes, floods) to estimate potential losses and set appropriate premiums and capital.

Subrogation
Claims

The right of an insurer to pursue a third party that caused an insurance loss to the insured. After paying a claim, the insurer "steps into the shoes" of the insured.

The Float
Investment · Finance

Premiums collected that are held before claims are paid. Warren Buffett famously uses Berkshire's float as essentially free capital to invest — the engine of insurance as a business model.

Facultative Reinsurance
Reinsurance

Risk-by-risk reinsurance placement, where the reinsurer can accept or reject each individual risk. Used for large, unique, or hazardous exposures outside treaty limits.

Retrocession
Reinsurance

When a reinsurer passes on risk to another reinsurer (a retrocessionaire). The deepest layer of the global risk transfer chain.
